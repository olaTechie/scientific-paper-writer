# Prose Conventions Reference

This document is the comprehensive prose style guide for all manuscript output
generated by the scientific-paper-writer plugin. The core engine (SKILL.md)
provides a summary; this file contains the full rules.

---

## 1. Tense

Use tense consistently within each section. The following table defines the
standard tense for each major manuscript section.

| Section | Tense | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Methods | Past | "Participants were randomised to receive either the intervention or placebo." |
| Results | Past | "The pooled odds ratio was 1.45 (95% CI 1.12 to 1.89; p = 0.004)." |
| Discussion (current study) | Past | "Our analysis demonstrated a significant association between exposure and outcome." |
| Discussion (established knowledge) | Present | "Cardiovascular disease remains the leading cause of mortality worldwide." |
| Discussion (implications) | Present / Future | "These findings suggest that early intervention may reduce long-term morbidity." |
| Introduction (background) | Present | "Type 2 diabetes affects approximately 462 million individuals globally." |
| Introduction (gap/rationale) | Present perfect / Past | "Previous studies have not examined this association in paediatric populations." |
| Captions | Present | "Figure 1 shows the PRISMA flow diagram for study selection." |
| Abstract | Past (methods/results), Present (conclusions) | "We included 34 studies. The pooled effect suggests benefit." |

### Tense Exceptions

- When describing a specific prior study's methods or findings in the Discussion
  or Introduction, past tense is acceptable: "Smith et al. reported a hazard
  ratio of 1.32."
- When stating a general scientific principle, present tense is always correct:
  "Heterogeneity reflects variability in effect estimates across studies."

## 2. Voice

### 2.1 General Guidance

Passive voice is standard in scientific writing and is acceptable throughout.
Active voice is preferred when it improves readability and reduces ambiguity.

| Voice | When to Use | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Passive | Default for Methods and Results | "Blood samples were collected at baseline and 12 weeks." |
| Active | When the agent matters or for clarity | "We excluded studies that did not report the primary outcome." |
| Active | Conclusions and recommendations | "Clinicians should consider this intervention for patients with..." |

### 2.2 Consistency Rule

Within any single subsection, maintain the same voice. Do not alternate between
active and passive voice in consecutive sentences unless there is a clear
reason (e.g., shifting from the authors' actions to a general process).

### 2.3 Avoid First-Person Overuse

Use "we" sparingly. Limit first-person constructions to statements where
authorial agency is relevant: "We hypothesised...", "We chose this threshold
because...", "We excluded...". For routine procedural descriptions, prefer
passive voice.

## 4. Precision

Scientific writing demands exact reporting. The following table shows common
vague expressions and their precise replacements.

### 4.1 "Instead of X, Write Y" Table

| Instead of... | Write... |
|---|---|
| "most studies" | "28 of 34 studies (82.4%)" |
| "a significant increase" | "an increase of 12.3 percentage points (95% CI 8.1 to 16.5; p < 0.001)" |
| "a large number of participants" | "4,523 participants across 12 studies" |
| "slightly higher" | "higher by 0.3 standard deviations (95% CI 0.1 to 0.5)" |
| "roughly half" | "17 of 34 studies (50.0%)" |
| "the results were similar" | "the pooled OR was 1.02 (95% CI 0.89 to 1.17; p = 0.78), suggesting no meaningful difference" |
| "a few patients experienced adverse events" | "adverse events occurred in 23 of 456 participants (5.0%)" |
| "outcomes improved" | "the mean score improved by 4.2 points (SD 1.8; p = 0.003)" |

### 4.2 Quantification Rules

| Metric | Decimal Places | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Percentages | 1 | 34.7% |
| Odds ratio (OR) | 2 | OR 1.45 |
| Hazard ratio (HR) | 2 | HR 0.78 |
| Risk ratio (RR) | 2 | RR 1.23 |
| Risk difference (RD) | 2-3 | RD 0.045 |
| Mean difference (MD) | Match source scale | MD 3.2 |
| Standardised mean difference (SMD) | 2 | SMD 0.45 |
| P-values | 2-3 significant digits | p = 0.034, p < 0.001 |
| I-squared (heterogeneity) | 1 | I^2 = 45.3% |
| Tau-squared | 2 | tau^2 = 0.12 |
| Number needed to treat (NNT) | 0 (whole number) | NNT = 12 |
| Sensitivity / Specificity | 1 | Sensitivity 89.4% |
| AUC / C-statistic | 2 | AUC 0.85 |

### 4.3 Confidence Interval Formatting

- In running text: "(95% CI 1.12 to 1.89)"
- Use "to" not an en-dash in text.
- In tables, en-dashes are acceptable: "1.12-1.89".
- Always state the confidence level: "95% CI" not just "CI".

## 5. Formatting Rules

### 5.1 Paragraphs Only

Manuscript sections must be written as continuous prose paragraphs. Never use:
- Bullet points
- Numbered lists
- Dashes as list markers

These are acceptable only in non-manuscript outputs (planning notes, checklists,
revision summaries).

### 5.2 Subsection Headings

Use `##` (Markdown H2) for subsection headings within major sections:
- `## Search Strategy`
- `## Study Selection`
- `## Data Extraction`
- `## Quality Assessment`
- `## Statistical Analysis`

### 5.3 Cross-References

- Refer to figures as "Figure 1", "Figure 2" (capitalised, unabbreviated).
- Refer to tables as "Table 1", "Table 2".
- Refer to supplementary materials as "eFigure 1", "eTable 2", "eMethods".
- Place cross-references in parentheses when they are not the grammatical
  subject: "The forest plot (Figure 2) shows..."

### 5.4 Abbreviations

- Define at first use in each major section. Major sections are independent
  units for abbreviation purposes: Methods, Results, Discussion, Abstract,
  Introduction.
- Do not abbreviate terms used fewer than three times in a section.
- Standard abbreviations that need no definition: DNA, RNA, HIV, AIDS, BMI,
  WHO, CI, OR, HR, RR, SD, IQR, NNT, AUC, ROC.

### 5.5 Numbers

- Spell out zero through nine in general text.
- Use numerals for 10 and above.
- Always use numerals with units: "5 mg", "3 mL", "7 days".
- Always use numerals for statistical values: "3 studies", "5 participants".
- Never start a sentence with a numeral. Rephrase or spell out:
  - Incorrect: "34 studies met the inclusion criteria."
  - Correct: "Thirty-four studies met the inclusion criteria."

## 6. Common Errors

The following errors are frequently encountered in scientific manuscripts.
The assistant must avoid all of them.

| Error | Correction | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| "Data was collected..." | "Data were collected..." | "Data" is plural (singular: "datum"). |
| "...which met the inclusion criteria" | "...that met the inclusion criteria" | Use "that" for restrictive (defining) clauses; "which" for non-restrictive clauses (preceded by a comma). |
| "compared to the control group" | "compared with the control group" | "Compared with" for statistical/quantitative comparisons; "compared to" for analogies. |
| "The results were significant" | "The results were statistically significant (p = 0.034)" | "Significant" without qualification is ambiguous; always specify statistical significance and provide the p-value. |
| "This proves that..." | "This suggests / indicates / demonstrates that..." | Individual studies cannot "prove" a hypothesis; science operates through accumulation of evidence. |
| "This is a novel study" | "To our knowledge, this is the first study to..." | "Novel" is subjective and should be avoided; frame originality with appropriate hedging. |
| "Interestingly, the results..." | "The results showed..." | Editorialising adverbs ("interestingly", "surprisingly", "importantly") weaken scientific prose. Remove them. |
| "We found that X was higher than Y" | "X was higher than Y (mean difference 3.2, 95% CI 1.1 to 5.3; p = 0.003)" | Findings without quantification are incomplete. Always provide the effect size and CI. |
| "...due to the fact that..." | "...because..." | Wordy construction. Use concise alternatives. |
| "It is well known that..." | [Delete or cite] | If it is well known, cite the source. If no source exists, do not claim common knowledge. |
| "In order to..." | "To..." | Unnecessary wordiness. "To" is sufficient. |

## 7. Journal-Specific Overrides

When a target journal is specified in PAPER_CONTEXT.md, the following defaults
may be overridden:

| Rule | Override Condition |
|---|---|
| UK English | US English if journal requires it (e.g., JAMA, NEJM, Annals of Internal Medicine) |
| Structured abstract | Unstructured if journal requires it |
| Word limits | Adjusted per journal guidelines |
| Reference style | Vancouver, APA, or journal-specific format |
| Figure resolution | Minimum DPI per journal requirements |

The assistant must flag any conflict between the default rules and journal
requirements: `[PLEASE VERIFY: journal style requires US English — switching
from UK English default]`.
